After solving some of the clues, we can notice that there are many pairs of words which only differ by one letter, and there are also many words that combine to make two-word phrases. It turns out that the pieces within each group can be connected such that all horizontal connections make two-word phrases or compound words, and all vertical connections differ by exactly one letter (just like a word ladder). For example, in the first section, {Certain Starbucks size}=TALL connects to {Status or reputation}=STANDING on its west edge, {Instruction}=ORDER on its east edge, and {____ effect (physical phenomenon involving magnetism)}=HALL on its south edge.

Additionally, each group of clues is missing exactly one piece, and the word on this piece can always be uniquely deduced from the adjacent words (and in all cases, there are exactly 2 adjacent words). For example, when we put together all the pieces in the first group, there is nothing that connects on the east side of {Certain precipitation}=RAIN, as well as the west side of {Friend in Australia}=MATE. We can fill in this space with CHECK, which makes two-word phrases with RAIN on the west side and MATE on the east.

Within each group, the boxes and connectors form a letter shape (for most of the groups the letter is pretty clear with only the boxes, but in e.g. the fourth group, the connectors are needed to see the R shape). The groups are sorted by alphabetical order of their first clue, so we want to find a way to reorder these letters. This is where the missing pieces come in - these 20 pieces can be connected to form a big cycle, with horizontal and vertical connections working the same way as the rest of the puzzle. For example, the missing piece in the first group was CHECK with connectors on the east and west edges, which ends up joining with SPOT from the last group (connectors on the east and south edges) and BOX from the 11th group (connectors on the east and west edges).

Authors Notes

The original inspiration for this puzzle came from thinking about joining word ladders via two-word phrases. This went through a lot of brainstorming, including ideas about making a 3-dimensional structure through 3 kinds of connections, or something involving counting the number of ways to navigate through a set of ladders. Eventually I settled on this extraction since it could potentially make for a satisfying do-it-again step, though it did make the puzzle larger than originally planned.
